# Biospecimen-Participation-Analysis
Colon cancer clinical trial analysis using SAS to evaluate how patient demographics and site factors influence consent and tissue submission rates.
# Overview
This project investigates factors influencing patient consent and tissue submission in a colon cancer clinical trial using SAS. The analysis evaluates whether demographic variables (race, age, sex, income) and clinical site characteristics are associated with participation, with the goal of identifying potential disparities in research representation and sample availability. 
# Objectives
Analyze patient consent rates in a clinical trial setting  
Evaluate tissue submission rates among participants  
Assess the impact of demographic variables (race, age, sex, income)  
Examine variation across clinical sites  
Identify potential disparities in participation and sample contribution  
# Data
This project uses a mock dataset created for analytical and educational purposes.  
The dataset was generated by the author and does not contain real patient data.  
No protected health information (PHI) is included.  
The dataset is not publicly shared as part of this repository.  
The structure of the dataset includes:  
Patient demographics (age, race, sex, income)  
Clinical site identifiers  
Biospecimen consent status  
Biospecimen tissue submission status  
# Methods
Analyses performed in SAS  
Univariable and multivariable logistic regression models  
Descriptive statistics to summarize consent and submission rates  
Comparative analysis across demographic groups  
Evaluation of differences between clinical sites  
Outcomes:  
Consent (yes/no)  
Submission (yes/no)  
Covariates: race, age, sex, study, ADI, median income, enrollment year  
# Key Findings
The results show substantial attrition across participation steps.      
Of 34,446 analyzed patients, only 21,106 consented (58%) and 10,718 (29%) submitted biospecimens.  
Race is the strongest predictor of participation.  
Black patients had significantly lower odds of:  
Consent (OR = 0.39) (p < 0.0001)  
Submission (OR = 0.51) (p < 0.0001)  
These disparities persisted after adjusting for socioeconomic and demographic factors.  
Socioeconomic status influences participation.  
Higher deprivation (ADI) had a lower likelihood of consent (p < 0.01)    
Higher income had a higher likelihood of consent (p < 0.01)  
Effects were less consistent in submission models.  
Older age is associated with lower participation.  
Increasing age was consistently linked to reduced odds of both consent and submission (p <0.0001)   
No meaningful differences by sex.  
Participation rates did not differ significantly between male and female patients.  
Declining submission over time.  
Later enrollment years were associated with lower likelihood of biospecimen submission (p < 0.01)    
# Key Insights
Biospecimen participation in this study is not random. It is skewed toward younger, higher-income, and White patients, with race emerging as the most significant and persistent driver of disparity. These patterns introduce potential selection bias and limit generalizability, particularly for underrepresented populations.
